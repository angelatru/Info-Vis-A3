Will we empty
the oceans by the next century?

Overfishing is one of the largest threats endangering the population of marine life. We are fishing so much that the ocean has trouble keeping up, unable to naturally replenish at the rate we are fishing. Fisheries around the world are continuously being pushed beyond limit, at the point of collapse for some. A major reason for overfishing becoming a global problem is due to poor fisheries management, and Illegal, Unreported and Unregulated fishing.

Fish Consumption & Trade

Let's look at the trend in the consumption of fish in the world and how it has changed overtime.

Overall, the world is eating more seafood per capita with the forerunners of consumption being Iceland, The Maldives, and Hong Kong in 2017.

With more demand for fish as well as technological advancements to how we catch fish, more workers are needed to supply for the demand. As such, there has been a boom in the industry of fisheries and aquaculture, increasing by 60% since 1995.

Aquaculture refers to the regulated growth (breeding) of aquatic animals and aquatic plants, using or within freshwater, sea water, brackish water and inland saline water. The growth of the world's aquaculture industry is hailed by East Asia and the Pacific, notably China, which in 2015 produced 61 million tonnes.

The growth in aquaculture over the millennia has made it possible to increase global seafood production without sacrificing fish stocks. Production continues to grow while the output of wild fishering has slowed, allowing us to maintain sustainable fishing levels that do not put fish populations at risk.

Despite the growth in aquaculture and sustainable breeding, it is still a fact that still today, illegal fishing within fisheries - which focus on wild catch or producing and collecting fish - continue to be the driving factor of depleting fish stocks across the world.

Fish Stocks & Health


As of 2017, 34% of global fish stocks are overfished.


Sustainable fishing refers to catching fish at a pace where the stock can naturally reproduce without reducing population, resulting in being able to catch as many fish as possible. Overfishing on the other hand is where we catch fish faster than they can reproduce. In turn, reducing populations and depleting stocks gradually as we fish at a rigorous pace. Overfishing is more prevalent in wild catch fisheries as the management of fisheries around the world is not well overseen.


Health of fish stocks refers to the biomass of certain fish stocks, essentially, the amount of wild fish in the ocean. The Maximum Sustainable Yield is known as the "sweet spot" for catching fish whereby the rate of reproduction and catch is equal. The value of one in the chart is optimal for the "sweet spot". A value below one means that the fish stocks are lower than the rate we are catching them, resulting in a potential gradual decline in fish population.

The Good News

Although the sustainability of global fishery resources continues to decline, the decline is at a much reduced state than the past. Improved regulation and effective monitoring of Illegal, Unreported and Unregulated fishing has proven successful in reverting overfished populations back to sustainable levels - as seen from recent tuna stocks. Unfortunately, the adoption of these regulations have been slow, particularly in developing countries.

These better management of fisheries that have been prevalent in the last millenia positively impacted the total fish stocks of the world. There is an upward trend in multiple fish health and stocks - particularly tuna -, showcasing the progression towards sustainable fishing . On average, scientifically-assessed fish populations around the world are healthy or improving. And, for fish populations that are not doing well, there is a clear roadmap to sustainability.

What Can You Do?

Organisations across the globe are continuously taking on the necessity of sustainable fishery management, keeping the oceans ample with fish. What you can do is be aware and choose certified sustainable seafood such as the Marine Stewardship Council (MSC) and Aquaculture Stewardship Council (ASC). You can also help organisations directly by taking action alongside them such as volunteering or help fund them by donating what you can.
